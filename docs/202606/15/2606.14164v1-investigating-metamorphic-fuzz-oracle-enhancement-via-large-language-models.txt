ZHENYU CHEN ∗, State Key Laboratory for Novel Software Technology, China Fuzz drivers are important components of greybox fuzzing; they encapsulate the target interface of the tested library, define the test space, and largely determine the outcomes of fuzzing. Fuzz drivers rely on (fuzz) oracles to detect potential bugs. As a common practice, existing fuzz drivers typically focus on security testing and adopt program crashes as the oracle. Although general and practical, this practice overlooks the functionality of the library under test, thereby restricting the bug-mining capability of greybox fuzzing. In this paper, we present the first study on metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement (MFOE), which aims to improve existing fuzz drivers by incorporating metamorphic-based oracles. The metamorphic-based oracles are grounded on metamorphic relations (MRs), which reflect the expected relationships between multiple inputs and their corresponding outputs. However, both the construction and integration of metamorphic-based oracles require a deep understanding of the fuzz target, making automatic MFOE particularly challenging. Modern large language models (LLMs), with their advanced capabilities in code understanding and generation, provide a promising opportunity to overcome this obstacle, inspiring us to develop an LLM-based framework. We name the LLM-based MFOE framework as MetaFOE and extensively evaluate it on fuzz drivers selected from OSS-Fuzz. To conduct a comprehensive investigation, we adopt three modern LLMs and five prompt strategies to configure MetaFOE. In total, we generate 3,475 MRs, 77.3% of which are applicable. Building on these MRs, we implement 12,351 meta drivers (i.e., drivers incorporating metamorphic-based oracles), of which 6,228 are valid. After three hours of fuzzing, these valid meta drivers achieve an average 18.7% improvement in edge coverage and triggered 1,528 unique crashes. Our study highlights the necessity of incorporating fuzz drivers with metamorphic-based oracles and demonstrates the feasibility of an LLM-based automatic MFOE, offering valuable insight to the fuzzing community. 1 Introduction 

Greybox fuzzing is a simple yet super effective automatic testing technique [Böhme et al . 2025; Zhu et al . 2022]. With a favorable trade-off between effectiveness and efficiency, greybox fuzzing has become an important testing capability, empowering notable bug-hunting services [Serebryany 2017; Syzbot 2026] and leading to the discovery of tens of thousands of software vulnerabilities [Google 2026; Zalewski 2026]. The fuzz driver (or fuzz harness 1) is a vital component of greybox fuzzing, especially for domains such as library testing [LLVM-LibFuzzer 2026; Zhang et al . 2024b]. Typically, a fuzz driver functions as the entry point for test executions; it not only encapsulates the units to be tested (e.g., library APIs) but also delimits the reachable code regions, which largely determine the results of fuzzing [Galland and Böhme 2025; Lyu et al . 2024]. Substantial manual efforts have been invested in developing and maintaining fuzz drivers [Görz et al . 2025; OSS-Fuzz 2026c], and various automated techniques have been proposed along this line of research [Babić et al . 2019; Chen et al . 2023; Jeong et al . 2023; Liu et al . 2025a; Toffalini et al . 2025]. All of these advances underscore the importance of crafting high-quality fuzz drivers. Although numerous works have been devoted to driver generation, they mainly concentrate on the synthesis of test sequences (i.e., ordered and structured API calls [Sherman and Nagy 2025]) while largely overlooking the construction of fuzz oracles . In software testing, an oracle is the mechanism used to determine whether an observed program behavior is a bug [Barr et al . 2014]; and we refer to those employed in fuzzing as fuzz oracles [Manes et al . 2018]. To understand the current status of the fuzz oracles used in existing drivers, we conduct a systematic inspection (detailed results are in §5.1) of the source code of 1,068 drivers from OSS-Fuzz [OSS-Fuzz 2026c]. We find that 63.9% of the drivers do not implement any dedicated bug-detection mechanisms and rely solely on program crashes to indicate bugs. Among the remaining drivers, 50.1% only implement “pseudo oracles” to check the validity of test inputs and the drivers themselves. Only 18.9% of the inspected drivers implement functional oracles that target the libraries under test, highlighting the critical need to improve existing fuzz oracles. In this paper, we conduct the first study investigating the enhancement of fuzz oracles, in particular those embedded in existing drivers. In the prior art, researchers have recognized the shortcomings of fuzz oracles and have proposed novel techniques to tackle this problem [Han and Zhou 2020; Serebryany et al . 2012; Zhang et al . 2021, 2022]. As a powerful technique for alleviating the oracle problem [Guo et al . 2024; Liu et al . 2013; Mu et al . 2025; Rigger and Su 2020; Segura et al .2018], metamorphic testing has naturally attracted the attention of the fuzzing community and has been adopted to enhance fuzz oracles. For example, Pan et al . [2024] summarize the characteristics of excessive data exposures (EDEs) and design novel oracles to fuzz and expose EDEs in real-world web applications. Kokkonis et al . [2025] identify the symptoms of code-level backdoors and construct a metamorphic (test) oracle to assist AFL++ [AFL++Team 2026] in detecting run-time backdoor triggers. These works (which can be generally termed metamorphic fuzzing ) have proposed insightful solutions to the fuzz oracle problem, yet only concentrate on constructing

oracles to expose specific types of bugs (i.e., EDEs and code-level backdoors) and do not address the lack of well-crafted oracles in existing drivers, which motivates us to explore this issue. Inspired by the success of metamorphic fuzzing, we propose improving existing fuzz drivers with metamorphic oracles. We refer to this pipeline as metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement (MFOE) and identify two essential tasks of it: (1) Metamorphic Relation Generation (MRG) . Analyzing fuzzed libraries to extract metamor-phic relations (MRs) [Chen et al . 2018], which are the core of metamorphic testing and the foundation for constructing metamorphic oracles. (2) Fuzz Oracle Implementation (FOI) . Analyzing given fuzz drivers, matching them with the generated MRs, and implementing appropriate oracles to construct enhanced drivers. Both of MRG and FOI require a deep understanding of the input software assets (i.e., fuzzed libraries and fuzz drivers), which poses a tricky obstacle to MFOE. In recent years, modern large language models (LLMs) have advanced substantially and shown strong capabilities in code generation and understanding [Shang et al . 2025; Sun et al . 2025; Valentin et al . 2026], which opens opportunities to mitigate the obstacle to MFOE. Moreover, pioneer researchers have demonstrated that LLMs can be applied to the generation of fuzz drivers [Lyu et al . 2024; Zhang et al . 2024b] and MRs [Shin et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2025b], which inspires us to develop an LLM-based workflow for MFOE. We name the LLM-based MFOE pipeline as MetaFOE and conduct extensive experiments to gain an empirical understanding of it. Specifically, we systematically evaluate the capabilities of LLMs in (1) generating valid MRs for given fuzz drivers and target libraries, and (2) constructing metamorphic oracles based on valid MRs and improving given fuzz drivers; in this evaluation, three representative LLMs are adopted, and five mainstream prompting strategies are investigated. For each generated MR, we manually validate it following pioneering work [Zhang et al . 2025b]. For each enhanced driver candidate produced by an LLM, we verify its validity through a three-phase process with checks at both the compilation and runtime levels. With 10 original fuzz drivers and the corresponding project documents as raw input, MetaFOE generates 3,475 MRs and 12,351 meta drivers in total, of which 77.3% and 50.4% are applicable and valid. For the 6,228 valid meta drivers, we further evaluate their fuzzing capabilities (i.e., code coverage and crash detection) through three-hour campaigns. The results show that the meta drivers achieve 18.7% more edge coverage and trigger 1,528 unique crashes. These results reveal that metamorphic oracles can not only improve code coverage but also crash detection. We also experiment MetaFOE using three LLMs and five prompt strategies to evaluate its sensitivity to different configurations. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

• Novel Pipeline . We propose a novel task named metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement, and develop an LLM-based pipeline MetaFOE to realize it. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to improve fuzz oracles encoded in existing drivers. 

• Systematic Study . We conduct a systematic study of existing fuzz oracles by inspecting 1,068 drivers from OSS-Fuzz. We also perform extensive experiments to evaluate (1) MetaFOE’s perfor-mance in MRG and FOI, which consume 213 M tokens; and (2) the fuzzing capability of valid meta drivers, which spends more than two CPU years. 

• Enhanced Drivers . Through extensive experiments, MetaFOE generates 6,228 valid meta drivers, which cover 18.7% more edges than original drivers on average, and trigger 1,528 crashes. We release these generated meta drivers in our artifact [MetaFOE-Team 2026].

2.1 Greybox Fuzzing 

Greybox fuzzing is a representative modern software security technique responsible for numerous bugs. Building on the basic fuzzing workflow, greybox fuzzing additionally leverages code coverage as the primary feedback. It continuously generates a large number of test inputs to repeatedly exercise fuzz targets (invoked through fuzz drivers), aiming to explore previously unseen code regions or trigger latent bugs via massive executions [Kokkonis et al. 2025; Qian et al. 2025]. 

Fuzz Driver. The fuzz driver is a critical component of greybox fuzzing that implements the execution entry point of the fuzz target (e.g., the library under test). In addition to the main step that invokes the Abstract Programming Interfaces (APIs) to be fuzzed, a qualified fuzz driver usually includes two other steps: (1) a preprocessing step to prepare test inputs for the tested interfaces, and (2) a postprocessing step to release the resources [LLVM-LibFuzzer 2026; Zhang et al . 2024b]. Fig. 1 illustrates two LibFuzzer-style drivers from the C-Blosc2 project; the code blocks that implement the preprocessing, main, and postprocessing steps are colored orange, green, and blue. In our study, we focus on fuzz oracles and dissect a driver into two parts: the test sequence 

and the test oracle . Specifically, the test sequence part defines the test logic of the tested APIs (i.e., the invocation order and parameter dependencies), and the test oracle part validates the execution results of the sequence to determine whether a bug is observed. The driver shown in Fig. 1a encompasses both the test sequence and the test oracle parts, where the explicit oracles 2

implemented with assert statements are highlighted in red. C-Blosc2 is a C library for data compression, and the assert oracles embedded in the driver checksum_fuzzer are designed to verify the equivalence and consistency of CRC32, which are fundamental building blocks in data compression. With such oracles, a driver can help fuzzing to detect not only plain bugs that lead to program crashes, but also functional bugs that violate the semantic correctness of critical APIs, such as CRC32 implemented in the C-Blosc2 library. However, unlike the assert oracles illustrated in Fig. 1a, the common practice in fuzzing still largely relies on program crashes (i.e., implicit oracles [Barr et al . 2014]) as the sole oracle. As shown in Fig. 1b, the driver fuzz_compress_chunk does not perform any explicit checks on the execution results; it just constructs inputs, invokes the target APIs (e.g., blosc1_set_compressor ), and frees the allocated memory, relying on repeated executions of this workflow during fuzzing to expose bugs. Such implicit oracles are overly simplistic and may miss important bugs, revealing limitations in existing fuzz oracles. This observation motivates us to carry out this study. 

OSS-Fuzz. OSS-Fuzz is a continuous fuzzing service provided by Google to support open-source projects [Google 2026; OSS-Fuzz 2026c]. With incentives in place, an open-source library can choose to participate in OSS-Fuzz for continuous fuzzing. To enable integration into the OSS-Fuzz infrastructure, library vendors should prepare standard artifacts mandated by OSS-Fuzz (e.g., integration configurations and fuzz drivers) before officially requesting inclusion [OSS-Fuzz 2026a]. To date, OSS-Fuzz has successfully integrated more than 1,000 projects, supporting the fuzzing of libraries written in mainstream languages such as C/C++, Java, and Python [OSS-Fuzz 2026b]. By aggregating abundant fuzzing assets (e.g., initial seed corpora, fuzz-exposed bugs, and fuzz drivers) from actively maintained open-source projects, OSS-Fuzz has become a valuable research testbed, facilitating a number of studies in areas such as regression fuzzing [Zhu and Böhme 2021], CI/CD fuzzing [Huang and Lemieux 2026; Sharma et al . 2024], and empirical fuzzing [Ding and Le Goues 2021; Zhang et al . 2024b]. Following this line of research, we adopt OSS-Fuzz as our primary data source and conduct this empirical study with the fuzz oracles integrated in OSS-Fuzz.

2.2 Metamorphic Testing 

Metamorphic testing was first proposed by Chen et al . [1998] to address the test generation problem. During the past few decades, it has evolved into a prominent remediation of the test oracle problem [Chen et al . 2018]. Given an algorithm 𝑓 (i.e., the design specification) and a program 𝑝 that implements this algorithm, metamorphic testing aims to extract a set of metamorphic relations (MRs) 𝑅 = {R 1, R2, ..., R𝑚 } from 𝑓 and leverage them to validate whether 𝑝 is implemented correctly. Essentially, an MR R is a necessary property that the algorithm 𝑓 (and thus its implementation 

𝑝 ) should satisfy. If an input–output pair violates R on 𝑝 , then the output is deemed erroneous, indicating the presence of bugs in 𝑝 . Let 𝑥 denote a concrete input and 𝑓 (𝑥 ) denote the corresponding output produced by 𝑓 . Once an MR R = ⟨𝑥 1, 𝑥 2, . . . , 𝑥 𝑛 , 𝑓 (𝑥 1), 𝑓 (𝑥 2), . . . , 𝑓 (𝑥 𝑛 )⟩ is identified and established, metamorphic testing typically proceeds in four steps to generate test cases and validate the correctness of 𝑝 [Chen et al. 2018], as follows: 

• Construct R′ by replacing 𝑓 in R with 𝑝 , where R′ is the MR used to validate 𝑝 .

• Given a sequence of source test cases ⟨𝑥 1, 𝑥 2, . . . , 𝑥 𝑘 ⟩, execute the program 𝑝 on each test case to obtain the corresponding outputs ⟨𝑝 (𝑥 1), 𝑝 (𝑥 2), . . . , 𝑝 (𝑥 𝑘 )⟩ .

• Construct the follow-up test cases ⟨𝑥 𝑘 +1, 𝑥 𝑘 +2, . . . , 𝑥 𝑛 ⟩ according to R′, execute them to obtain the corresponding outputs ⟨𝑝 (𝑥 𝑘 +1), 𝑝 (𝑥 𝑘 +2), . . . , 𝑝 (𝑥 𝑛 )⟩ .

• Validate 𝑝 by checking whether the execution results, i.e., ⟨𝑝 (𝑥 𝑘 +1), 𝑝 (𝑥 𝑘 +2), . . . , 𝑝 (𝑥 𝑛 )⟩ , satisfy 

R′; if R′ is violated, then a bug in 𝑝 is revealed. In this study, we propose enhancing existing fuzz drivers by integrating metamorphic oracles, which are derived and implemented based on MRs. The programs under test are the libraries invoked by the fuzz drivers, with test generation handled by the fuzzer. The MRs should therefore be extracted from both the libraries under test and the associated drivers, and we address this task

3 Metamorphic-based Fuzz Oracle Enhancement 

Modern greybox fuzzing typically relies on the program crash—a type of implicit oracle [Barr et al .2014]—to identify bugs. This practice restricts the range of bugs that can potentially be detected, as crash-only oracles inherently overlook the functional semantics of the fuzz targets, i.e., the libraries bridged by the drivers. To address this limitation, we propose metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement (MFOE), a novel pipeline that augments existing fuzz drivers with metamorphic oracles extracted from the libraries under test. Let D = ⟨T , O𝑖 ⟩ be a fuzz driver constructed according to typical fuzzing practice, where O𝑖 

represents an implicit oracle that monitors unintended program crashes. The goal of MFOE is to modify D into D′ = ⟨T , O𝑒 ⟩ by replacing the original O𝑖 with an explicit oracle O𝑒 , which is constructed based on a metamorphic relation (MR). Drawing on the metamorphic testing workflow described in §2.2, we identify three essential tasks in MFOE, as follows: (1) MR Extraction . Let L be the library behind the fuzzer driver D. The MR extraction task is defined as a mapping 𝜌 (L) = 𝑅 , where 𝑅 = {R 1, R2, ..., R𝑛 } is the set of extracted MRs. (2) Oracle Instantiation . To function as a proper oracle (e.g., an assertion), an MR needs to be instantiated in the context of the fuzzer driver. Let D be a driver and R be an MR extracted from the library exercised by D. The oracle instantiation task is defined as a mapping 𝜙 (D , R) = O𝑒 

that crafts and outputs a metamorphic-based explicit oracle O𝑒 .(3) Oracle Integration . The last step of MFOE is to integrate the constructed oracle O𝑒 into the original driver D. As such, the oracle integration task is defined as a mapping 𝜓 (D , O𝑒 ) = D′,where D′ = ⟨T , O𝑒 ⟩ is the modified driver that incorporates a metamorphic-based oracle O𝑒 .In the rest of the paper, we refer to D′ as the meta driver since it is modified based on MRs. 

An LLM-based Implementation of MFOE. All three tasks outlined above require a deep understanding of the fuzzed libraries and the original drivers, posing significant challenges to implementing MFOE. As LLMs have demonstrated extraordinary capabilities in code-relevant tasks [Chen et al . 2025; Liu et al . 2025a; Ni 2025; Shin et al . 2024; Sun et al . 2025; Zhang et al . 2024a], we are motivated to leverage LLMs to address challenges in MFOE. Specifically, we develop MetaFOE, an LLM-based framework that implements FOE in two phases. The first phase is metamorphic relation generation 

(MRG), which implements the mapping 𝜌 defined in the MR extraction task. The second phase is 

fuzz oracle implementation (FOI), which implements both the 𝜙 and 𝜓 mappings defined in the oracle instantiation and integration tasks. The reason for handling 𝜙 and 𝜓 together is that they share D

as input and rely on similar contextual information, such as invoked APIs, relevant variables, and verifiable results. We further elaborate on MetaFOE in §4.

4.1 MetaFOE 

Taking fuzz assets (e.g., fuzz drivers, seeds, and the source code and documentation of the libraries under test) as input, MetaFOE performs two working phases, namely MRG and FOI (described in §3), in sequence to modify given fuzz drivers. During this procedure, a semi-automatic evaluation is conducted to assess the generated MRs in terms of (1) uniqueness and (2) applicability , and an in-process evaluation is conducted to assess the meta drivers along three dimensions: (1) compilability ,(2) validity , and (3) fuzzing capabilities (i.e., code coverage and bug discovery). 

4.1.1 Metamorphic Relation Generation (MRG). In the MRG phase, we first prompt an LLM to synthesize MRs, typically producing multiple candidates. The detailed prompting process for MRG is described in §4.5.2. Since some of the generated MRs are redundant or not suitable for oracle construction [Luu et al . 2023; Zhang et al . 2025b], we take two steps to evaluate them and discard those that are inapplicable. To this end, we first deduplicate the MRs using a similarity-based method powered by a small language model 3. After that, we manually validate the applicability of the MRs following the methodology of pioneering work [Luu et al . 2023; Zhang et al . 2025b]. To ensure reliability, the manual validation is carried out by two authors who are experienced in metamorphic testing. We carefully analyze the features of the libraries under test and validate the corresponding MRs accordingly. An MR is kept only if both authors agree on its applicability. 

4.1.2 Fuzz Oracle Implementation (FOI). Based on the MRs retained from the MRG phase, the FOI phase first modifies the original drivers by implementing and injecting new oracles, and then checks the validity of the modified drivers. Fig. 3 provides a closer view of the workflow in FOI phase, which comprises four stages: (1) Prompt-driven Oracle Constrution . Given a prompt template, a fuzz driver, and an MR as input, we first craft concrete prompting instructions to elicit the LLM to generate candidate meta drivers. In addition to the input described above, the prompting can also take compilation error messages into account if any candidate meta driver has failed in a previous compilation. (2) Compilation-level Driver Validation . This stage aims to filter out meta drivers that cannot be compiled. As shown in previous studies [Gu et al . 2024; Schäfer et al . 2023; Zhang et al .2024b], LLMs often struggle to generate compilable code. To alleviate this limitation, a widely adopted approach is to iteratively reprompt LLMs to refine the outputs, where compilation error messages are typically used to guide the reprompting. Following this approach, we develop a “reprompt-and-recompile” iteration to produce as many compilable drivers as possible. We set the number of compilations to at most five to balance effectiveness and cost. (3) Runtime-level Driver Validation . This stage aims to further filter out meta drivers that are compilable yet invalid at runtime, since a valid meta driver is expected to be not only runnable but also stable. After compiling a meta driver, we first run it against seed inputs to ensure that it is runnable. A short-term fuzzing (set at five minutes) is then conducted to eliminate candidate drivers that (1) achieve too little coverage, as it often implies that a meta driver implements erroneous behaviors; or (2) trigger excessive crashes, as it often indicates false positives [Zhang et al . 2024b]. Specifically, we discard the drivers that (1) crash in more than 0.01% of the executions or (2) achieve less than 10% of the coverage of the original driver during short-term fuzzing. The coverage of the original driver is the average of five fuzz campaigns. (4) Fuzzing Capability Evaluation : We finally conduct a long-term fuzzing to evaluate how the meta drivers are capable in code coverage and bug detection. For every retained meta driver, we first perform a three-hour campaign to obtain raw fuzzing results. Based on the results, we then count the coverage achieved by the meta drivers and analyze the triggered crashes. 

4.2 Research Questions 

Guided by the roadmap shown in Fig. 2 and centered on the MetaFOE framework, we investigate five research questions (RQs), as detailed below. 

RQ1 (Existing Fuzz Oracles §5.1): Do existing fuzz drivers contain oracles? What types of oracles are typically implemented? What are the purposes of the existing oracles? Fuzz oracles are critical to the effectiveness of fuzzing; nevertheless, their significance is often overlooked, and their role has not been systematically studied. RQ1 aims to understand the current  

> , Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: June 2026. Investigating Metamorphic Fuzz Oracle Enhancement via Large Language Models 9

landscape of fuzzing oracles. To this end, we identify the types of existing fuzz oracles and examine them to understand the intended purposes behind their implementations. 

RQ2 (Evaluation of LLM-based MRG §5.2): Is LLM-based MRG feasible? How well can LLMs construct applicable MRs? To what extent can these MRs be used for oracle construction? MRs are the fundamental elements of conducting metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement (MFOE); their quality is crucial to the performance of the produced meta drivers. Previous work has explored applying LLMs to synthesize MRs [Luu et al . 2023; Zhang et al . 2025b], but their use in MFOE remains unexplored. To bridge this gap, we design RQ2 to evaluate how well LLMs can synthesize applicable MRs, with a particular focus on their suitability for oracle construction. 

RQ3 (Evaluation of LLM-based FOI §5.3): Is LLM-based FOI feasible? How well can LLMs craft valid meta drivers? Can these meta drivers uncover fresh coverage or detect new bugs? The ultimate goal of MFOE is to enhance existing fuzz drivers by leveraging oracles constructed with MRs. The resulting meta drivers are expected to be stronger than their original version, uncovering additional coverage and exposing potential bugs. RQ3 investigates this problem. We first report statistics on the proportions of meta drivers that achieve the compilation-time and runtime validity, and then evaluate the fuzzing capabilities of the valid meta drivers. 

RQ4 (LLM Sensitivity §5.4): Can MetaFOE work with different LLMs? How do different LLMs perform on the two tasks, i.e., MRG and FOI, of MetaFOE? The LLM is a core building block of MetaFOE and can influence the overall performance of MetaFOE. RQ4 is designed to evaluate the sensitivity of MetaFOE to the concrete choice of LLM. We aim to understand how different LLMs affect the feasibility and quality of the generated MRs and meta drivers, providing insights into the robustness and generality of the framework. 

RQ5 (Prompt Sensitivity §5.5): Can MetaFOE work with different prompt strategies? Will the choice of prompt strategy impacts the results of MRG and FOI? The widespread adoption of LLMs is attributed not only to their strong language processing capabilities, but also to the flexibility of prompting, which requires no extensive data collection or additional training [Nong et al . 2025]. Previous studies [Sun et al . 2025; Zhang et al . 2024b] show that the choice of prompt strategy can significantly affect LLM performance in specific tasks, underscoring the need for a careful prompt design. Since MetaFOE also prompts to tame LLMs, we design RQ5 to systematically investigate how MetaFOE performs with different prompt strategies.

4.4 Large Language Models 

In this study, we select three representative LLMs; their details are as follows. 

GLM-4-Plus [Zhipu-AI 2026]. GLM-4-Plus is a large language model developed by Zhipu AI [Du et al . 2022]. It is designed for general-purpose language understanding and generation tasks, including code generation, reasoning, and conversational assistance [GLM et al . 2024]. Owing to its strong performance on code-related tasks, the GLM series has been widely adopted in both academic research and industrial applications, serving as a core model for software engineering tasks such as fuzzing [Fu et al . 2024; Wang et al . 2024] and software development [Du et al . 2024]. 

DeepSeek-V3.2 [DeepSeek 2026]. DeepSeek-V3.2 is a reasoning-oriented LLM developed by DeepSeek AI [Liu et al . 2025b]; it has exhibited strong multilingual understanding and competitive performance in code generation and completion in multiple programming languages. Given its effectiveness in program synthesis, bug detection, refactoring, and documentation generation, the DeepSeek-V3 series has drawn increasing attention in academia, advancing research in intelligent software engineering [Shen et al. 2026; Xia et al. 2025; Zeng et al. 2026]. 

GPT-4.1 [OpenAI 2025b]. GPT-4.1 is a recent model in the GPT-4 family developed by OpenAI [OpenAI 2025a]. Recognized as one of the most influential LLM families, the GPT series has been widely adopted in both academia and industry, supporting a broad range of applications in software engineering and security [Meng et al . 2024; Sapia and Böhme 2026; Xia et al . 2025; Yang et al .2024, 2025; Zhang et al . 2025a]. As the successor to GPT-4o, GPT-4.1 is more powerful and offers improved coding performance and long-context capabilities; we thus include it in our study. 

LLM Settings. In addition to the model and the prompt strategy, temperature is another key factor that can significantly affect LLM outputs [Xia et al . 2025; Zhang et al . 2024b]. To ensure reproducible results while maintaining moderate output variability, we set the temperature to 0.2 for all studied LLMs in both MRG and FOI. All other generation parameters are kept at their default values to avoid introducing additional tuning bias. 

4.5 Prompting Method 

In this section, we first describe the prompt strategies adopted in this study and then present the specific prompting process designed for MRG and FOI. 

4.5.1 Prompt Strategies. In this paper, we study five prompt strategies, namely zero-shot [Kojima et al . 2022], few-shot [Brown et al . 2020], crit ique [Madaan et al . 2023], expert [Ouyang et al .2022], and chain-of-thought [Wei et al . 2022]. We choose these prompt strategies because they are representative and widely adopted in fuzzing for driver generation [Zhang et al . 2024b], mutator synthesis [Ou et al . 2024], and input generation [Meng et al . 2024]. These strategies are also applied to other software engineering tasks, such as metamorphic testing [Shin et al . 2024; Zhang et al .2025b], unit test generation [Cheng et al . 2025; Gu et al . 2024], and automatic program repair [Yin et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2023]. The details of the studied prompt strategies are as follows. 

Zero-shot ( ZERO ). The zero-shot prompt directly instructs an LLM to perform a task without any task-specific examples, relying solely on its pre-trained knowledge. In this study, we design 

ZERO as simple instructions to serve as a basic baseline, following the setup of prior studies that investigate LLMs [Sun et al. 2025]. 

Few-shot ( FEW ). In contrast to ZERO , which provides no task demonstrations in the instructions, the few-shot prompt provides a handful of examples to help the model better understand the task context [Geng et al . 2024]. The examples profile the expected input-output pairs, allowing LLMs to capture task intuitions more accurately. We include FEW to investigate whether LLMs achieve better performance in MRG and FOI when provided with a small number of examples.

Critique ( CRIT ). The critique prompt is designed to elicit evaluative feedback from an LLM; it asks the model to review, critique, or refine an existing generation by pointing out errors or suggesting improvements [Sun et al. 2025]. In this study, we employ CRIT prompts that explicitly point out errors in the LLM’s outputs to specific tasks, such as the inapplicable MRs produced in MRG and invalid meta drivers constructed in FOI. 

Expert ( EXP ). The expert prompt instructs the LLM to assume the role of a domain expert, which biases its output toward specialized knowledge and style [Xu et al . 2023]. In this study, we prepare the four other prompts with minimal expert-guided instructions, such as “ you are a software testing expert familiar with metamorphic testing ” and “ you are an expert in fuzz testing ”, to ensure that the LLM can handle MFOE tasks properly. In addition to these basic instructions, we provide restrictive instructions to guide the LLM to focus more on specific tasks (i.e., MRG and FOI). Specifically, we ask an LLM to (1) understand MRG or FOI by dissecting it into concrete steps and (2) generate a description of an expert competent for the given task from a second-person perspective. 

Chain-of-thought ( COT ). The chain-of-thought prompt encourages the LLM to explicitly artic-ulate intermediate reasoning steps, helping the model better understand the task and thus improve its performance [Wei et al . 2022]. To construct appropriate COT templates for MRG and FOI, we summarize general workflows from prior studies on metamorphic testing [Chen et al . 2018] and fuzz driver generation [Babić et al . 2019], and instantiate them as structured operation chains. Details of the COT prompts for MRG and FOI are provided in §4.5.2 and §4.5.3, respectively.

Third, for the remaining IO relations, we instruct the LLM to convert them into MRs according to the format defined in the ZERO template.

5.1 RQ1: Existing Fuzz Oracles 

Finding 1: Existing fuzz oracles can be classified into four types, namely None, Assert, Abort, and Sanitizer. Among the 1,068 drivers that we collect from OSS-Fuzz, the four oracle types account for 63.9%, 18.3%, 16.1%, and 1.8%, respectively. These observations highlight the critical need for (automatic) fuzz oracle enhancement.

6 Discussion 

This section presents (1) a case study on crash analysis and LLM-assisted false-positive mitigation, (2) lessons learned, and (3) threats to validity. 

6.1 Case Study 

Fig. 8 illustrates a meta driver constructed in Libyaml (S06). The original driver is shown in the subfig (a), the implemented MR (MR-021) in (b), and the core MR-related code in the meta driver in (c). As shown, the original driver implements a basic test sequence, which initializes the test input, invokes the core API under test ( yaml_parse_initialize ), and releases resources after fuzzing. MR-021 

captures a whitespace-invariance property: appending trailing whitespace to a YAML document should not change its parsing results, i.e., both the event sequence and resulting data structures remain identical. The meta driver incorporates MR-021 by appending trailing whitespace (i.e., three spaces, two tabs, and four newlines) to the input, re-parsing it, and verifying the equivalence of structured event sequences between the original and transformed executions. The core function of the meta driver is collect_events , which parses a given YAML input and collects the resulting sequence of parsing events into a structured array for subsequent comparison; the source code is shown in Fig. 8(d), with the MR-supporting code in blue. At first glance, MetaFOE correctly implements the meta driver, with a valid MR and functional metamorphic testing logic. 

6.1.1 Meta-driver-induced False Positives. Through three-hour fuzzing, the meta driver shown in Fig. 8 triggers 141 crashes in total, all of which are assertion failures. After deduplication based on crash summaries, we observe that all crashes in fact originate from a single unique assertion failure (AF), which corresponds to a violation of the scalar_value check enforced by the assert 

statement highlighted in red. We further analyze this AF by examining the YAML specification and consulting ChatGPT, and conclude that it is a false positive. The failure stems from an invalid MR assumption rather than a Libyaml bug. Appending trailing whitespace can alter the parsed content of block scalars (e.g., “ |” and “ >”) without a terminating newline 4 , under which MR-021 

does not hold, leading to false positives. This observation indicates that, although LLMs are trained on large corpora and are capable of code understanding and test generation, they still cannot cover all corner cases and may therefore fail to implement fully correct metamorphic-based oracles. 

6.1.2 Mitigating False Positives. We explore mitigating false positives with the help of ChatGPT. To this end, we ask ChatGPT to refine the meta driver and provide it with three inputs: (1) the meta driver shown along with MR-021 , (2) the root cause description of the false positive (i.e., how the MR may not hold for YAML block scalars), and (3) a example crashing input generated during fuzzing. ChatGPT returns a patching plan that restricts the transformation to inputs ending with “ \n ”, and generates a refined meta driver. This ensures proper YAML termination, preventing appended whitespace from affecting scalar content and allowing MR-021 to hold for the assert highlighted in blue. The refined patch is shown in the upper-right corner of Fig. 8(c). A three-hour fuzzing of the refined meta driver still triggers 162 crashes, all of which originate from the blue-highlighted 

assert statement. This result is unexpected and motivates a deeper inspection of the generated inputs. We observe that, although false positives persist, the crashing inputs no longer exhibit the same pattern (i.e., ending with “ >” or “ |”) as those from the original meta driver, indicating that the patch is effective. This suggests that the false positives are only partially mitigated, with the addressed cases largely corresponding to the provided example inputs (i.e., those ending with “ >”or “ |”). Overall, while LLM-based refinement can mitigate false positives, it may require multiple iterations with diverse crashing inputs. We leave this for future work.

6.2 Lessons Learned 

We summarize the following lessons that may be helpful to the fuzzing community. 

(1) Fuzz Drivers Need Functional Oracles. Our analysis of 1,068 shows that 81.1% of OSS-Fuzz drivers lack functional oracles. Unlike implicit crash oracles, functional oracles require a deep understanding of the target library and are difficult to implement, especially for non-vendor users. We thus call for better oracle design from vendors and highlight the need for automated techniques (such as our approach MetaFOE) to support fuzz oracle construction. 

(2) LLMs are Capable in MFOE. Our experiments with three LLMs and five prompt strategies demonstrate that LLMs are feasible for realizing MFOE. Better performance can be achieved with more capable models (e.g., DeepSeek-V3.2) and mixed prompt strategies (e.g., COT +EXP ), as different prompts excel at different aspects. We hope these findings can inform future research. 

(3) LLM-generated Meta Drivers Need Refinement. Although LLMs are capable of generating applicable MRs and valid meta drivers, the resulting meta drivers may still suffer from false positives even after multi-stage validation. Our case study shows that LLMs can provide refinement patches

to mitigate such issues, suggesting the need for an additional refinement stage to iteratively improve the quality of generated meta drivers. We leave this to future work. 

6.3 Threats to Validity 

Internal Validity. Internal validity correlates with the completeness and fairness of the methodology [Ampatzoglou et al . 2019; Görz et al . 2025]. In our study, threats to internal validity mainly arise from the generation and evaluation of MRs and modified drivers. We address these threats by (1) constructing MFOE workflows based on established studies in metamorphic testing [Chen et al . 2018; Zhang et al . 2025b], fuzz driver generation [Zhang et al . 2024b], and LLM for software engineering [Sun et al . 2025], and by (2) adopting and refining evaluation metrics from these pioneering works. These efforts help to ensure internal validity to a large extent. 

External Validity. External validity refers to the extent to which the results can be generalized to subjects beyond those included in the study [Ampatzoglou et al . 2019; Görz et al . 2025]. In this paper, we (1) investigate various fuzz drivers from OSS-Fuzz and (2) include multiple LLMs (§4.4) and prompting strategies (§4.5). We also perform fuzzing for 18,684 CPU hours to evaluate the capabilities of the valid meta drivers. These efforts strengthen the external validity. 

7 Related Work 

Fuzz Driver Generation. Fuzz drivers are essential components of fuzzing and a growing body of work has been devoted to its automatic generation [Chen et al . 2023; Jeong et al . 2023; Lyu et al . 2024; Sherman and Nagy 2025]. Recent studies continue to tackle the open challenges of automatic driver generation; examples include WildSync [Wu et al . 2025] and LibErator [Toffalini et al . 2025]. With the advancement of modern LLMs, intelligent driver generation techniques have also emerged [Liu et al . 2025a; Zhang et al . 2024b]. In contrast to prior work that focuses on synthesizing call sequences and expanding API coverage, our study shifts attention to oracles, the critical components responsible for bug detection. Note that although Sherman and Nagy [2025] propose an oracle-guided technique, the “oracle” in there refers to evaluation criteria for driver quality rather than the bug-detection mechanism, making our study fundamentally different. 

Metamorphic Testing. Metamorphic testing is one of the most prominent techniques for alleviating the oracle problem [Barr et al . 2014; Howden 2006] and has addressed intricate challenges in testing complex or non-traditional software systems [Guo et al . 2024; Le et al . 2014; Mu et al . 2025]. It has also attracted significant interest from the fuzzing community and has been integrated into fuzzing techniques as an extended mechanism to detect bugs beyond easily observable crashes [Kokkonis et al . 2025; Liu et al . 2023; Pan et al . 2024; Schloegel et al . 2024; Tsigkanos et al . 2023]. In contrast to these studies, our study focuses on incorporating metamorphic-based oracles into fuzz drivers and is not limited to specific types of bugs. Researchers have explored the use of LLMs to support metamorphic testing. Pioneering groups include Tsigkanos et al . [2023], Shin et al . [2024], and Zhang et al . [2025b]. Inspired by this line of work, our study employs LLMs to generate MRs and construct stronger fuzz oracles, extending the pioneering efforts. 

LLM-based Testing. Modern LLMs have significantly reshaped software engineering (SE) [Abra-hamsson et al . 2017; He et al . 2025; Wei 2024]. Many SE tasks, such as code summarization [Crupi et al . 2025; Virk et al . 2025] and program repair [Bouzenia et al . 2025; Xie et al . 2025], have explored leveraging LLMs to improve effectiveness, intelligence, and automation. In addition to fuzzing and metamorphic testing, LLMs have also been applied to other fields of automatic testing [Harman et al . 2025]. For example, Shang et al . [2025] conduct a systematic study on the adoption of LLMs in unit testing. Ling et al . [2025] employ LLMs to test report clustering and facilitate crowdsourced

8 Conclusion 

In this paper, we investigate current practices in fuzz oracle implementation by analyzing 1,068 drivers collected from OSS-Fuzz. Our results show that 81.1% of the drivers lack functional oracles for bug detection, highlighting the need for stronger fuzz oracles. Motivated by this observation, we develop an LLM-based pipeline that leverages modern LLMs to augment existing fuzz drivers with metamorphic-based oracles. Through systematic experiments with three LLMs and five prompting strategies, we generate 2,685 applicable MRs and 6,228 valid meta drivers, demonstrating the feasibility of metamorphic-based fuzz oracle enhancement. A fuzzing exceeding two CPU years of the meta drivers shows that the meta drivers improve edge coverage by 18.7% over the original drivers and detect 1,528 unique crashes. Finally, we perform a case study to analyze the detected crashes, and distill actionable insights for the fuzzing community.
