# LRAT-Catcher: Importing SAT Solver Certificates into Lean 4 by Reflection 

# Stefan Szeider 

Algorithms and Complexity Group, TU Wien, Vienna, Austria 

sz@ac.tuwien.ac.at 

Abstract 

SAT solvers settle combinatorial problems beyond the reach of interactive theo-rem provers and produce LRAT certificates for independent verification. We present LRAT-Catcher, a standalone, general-purpose tool that imports a DIMACS formula together with an LRAT certificate into Lean 4 as a theorem. LRAT-Catcher runs the formally verified LRAT checker from Lean core as compiled native code via reflec-tion. This scales to instances where Mathlib ’s explicit proof-term import exhausts memory. LRAT-Catcher also composes cube-and-conquer solving runs entirely inside Lean. Per-cube refutations are combined with a cover-completeness certificate, itself an LRAT proof, into a single unsatisfiability theorem. Verified encodings connect CNF-level results to the original combinatorial problems. We evaluate the tool against 

Mathlib ’s proof-term import and the external checker cake_lpr on establishing the Schur number S(4) = 44 and the Ramsey number R(4 , 4) = 18 as Lean theorems. 

Keywords. SAT certificates, LRAT , Lean 4, proof by reflection, cube-and-conquer, ver-ified encodings. 

# 1 Introduction 

Boolean satisfiability (SAT) solvers settle combinatorial questions that lie beyond the reach of interactive theorem provers. One such question is the value of the fifth Schur number [5]. When a solver reports a formula unsatisfiable, it emits a certificate in the DRAT format or its checker-friendly refinement LRAT [2]. An independent checker confirms the result without repeating the search. Such a verdict is convincing, but not a theorem inside a proof assistant. It cannot be named, reused as a lemma, or composed with other formal developments. Closing this gap means importing a certificate as a reusable theorem whose statement pins down exactly which formula was refuted. Two Lean developments already import LRAT certificates. The lrat_proof command of 

Mathlib turns a DIMACS formula and an LRAT certificate into a theorem by constructing an explicit proof term [12], but it does not implement the resolution asymmetric tautology (RAT) rule. Because it binds the entire formula into the proof term, its memory cost grows with the formula rather than the certificate. It therefore exhausts memory on instances that solvers routinely solve. Lean’s standard library, on the other hand, provides a formally verified LRAT checker with full RAT support, Std.Tactic.BVDecide.LRAT , developed by Böving et al. for the bit-blasting tactic bv_decide [1]. That checker can only be reached through bv_decide on bitvector goals and is not exposed as a standalone certificate import. Its checking performance has not been measured against external checkers. 

> arXiv:2607.00815v1 [cs.LO] 1 Jul 2026

A second line of work proves combinatorial theorems with a SAT solver in the loop. The Empty Hexagon development of Subercaseaux et al. [9] verifies the encoding in Lean but checks the certificate externally with cake_lpr [11] and asserts the resulting unsatisfiability as an axiom after a solve-and-check computation of roughly 3 CPU years. Kirchweger et al. [6] integrate SAT modulo symmetries with Lean for end-to-end verified graph generation, formalizing the symmetry-breaking reasoning inside Lean to certify graph non-existence. The end-to-end formal verification of Keller’s conjecture by Gallicchio et al. [3] comes closest to what we aim for. It pairs verified encodings with verified LRAT and substitution-redundancy checkers. It runs the entire pipeline inside Lean by reflection, evaluating the verified checkers directly as Lean computations rather than emitting a separate proof term. Its encodings are built especially for that proof, and the method is not offered as a reusable certificate-import bridge. Outside Lean, the verified checkers cake_lpr and Lammich’s Isabelle-verified UNSAT-certificate checker [7], together with the on-the-fly, signature-based ImpCheck [8], establish unsatisfiability with strong guarantees. They return, however, a verdict to the shell rather than a theorem that later formal proofs can cite. We present LRAT-Catcher as a standalone tool for importing a DIMACS formula to-gether with an LRAT certificate into Lean 4 as a theorem. Reflection-based certificate checking inside Lean is not itself new; the Keller development above already evaluates veri-fied checkers as Lean computations. Our contribution is to offer it as a general certificate-import tool, usable for any DIMACS formula and LRAT certificate at a user-chosen trusted base, rather than as a bespoke step in a single proof. LRAT-Catcher is built on the verified Lean-core checker and inherits its full RAT support and binary parsing. It runs the checker in compiled native code via reflection, which scales to certificates where the explicit-proof-term import exhausts memory. It offers a kernel-only checking mode as an option that trades speed for a trusted base of the Lean kernel alone. Beyond single refutations, LRAT-Catcher composes cube-and-conquer [4] runs entirely inside Lean. It combines the per-cube refutations with a cover-completeness certificate into one unsatisfiability theorem, without a trusted cover combinator; the cover-completeness certificate is itself an LRAT proof. Ver-ified encodings connect these CNF-level results back to the combinatorial problems they came from. We evaluate LRAT-Catcher on a ladder of pigeonhole formulas and on verified encod-ings of the Schur and Ramsey problems, whose largest instances establish the Schur number 

S(4) = 44 and the Ramsey number R(4 , 4) = 18 as Lean theorems. The evaluation com-pares the two reflection modes against cake_lpr and Mathlib ’s lrat_proof , locating the memory-exhaustion point of explicit-proof-term import and the cost of the kernel-only checking mode (Section 6). LRAT-Catcher is publicly available as open-source software. 1

# 2 Background 

We consider propositional formulas in conjunctive normal form. A literal is a propositional variable or its negation. A clause is a finite disjunction of literals. A formula is a finite conjunction of clauses. An assignment maps variables to truth values. An assignment 

satisfies a formula if it satisfies every clause. A formula is unsatisfiable if no assignment satisfies it. In Lean, a formula is a CNF value. The property we certify is cnf.Unsat : no assignment satisfies cnf .A SAT solver establishes unsatisfiability by emitting a certificate that an independent checker verifies without repeating the search. Solvers write certificates in the DRAT format 

> 1https://github.com/leansolving/lrat-catcher

2or its checker-friendly refinement LRAT [2]. LRAT attaches to every added clause the clause indices needed to validate it. Checking thus reduces to guided unit propagation rather than search. An added clause is justified either as a reverse unit propagation (RUP) step or, more generally, as a resolution asymmetric tautology (RAT) step. The RAT rule lets 

LRAT certify the preprocessing and inprocessing techniques that solvers such as CaDiCaL 

depend on. A certificate is a sequence of such steps that ends in the empty clause. These steps are interleaved with clause deletions. Lean core ships a formally verified LRAT checker in its standard library, Std.Tactic .BVDecide.LRAT . It was developed by Böving et al. [1] as part of the bit-blasting tactic 

> bv_decide

. It pairs a Boolean checking function with a soundness theorem: if the checker accepts a certificate π for a formula, the formula is unsatisfiable, 

check( cnf , π ) = true =⇒ cnf .Unsat .

The checker implements the full RAT rule and parses both the textual and binary LRAT en-codings. Böving et al. [1, §4.4] describe the verified checker and its binary LRAT parser; the full RAT rule and textual parsing are further capabilities of the Std.Tactic.BVDecide.LRAT 

module of Lean v4.30.0, on which LRAT-Catcher builds directly. Establishing check (cnf , π ) = true for a concrete formula and certificate means evaluating the checking function. This is a proof by reflection : rather than build an explicit proof term, one evaluates a verified decision procedure and lets its result discharge the goal. Lean carries out the evaluation during elaboration , the phase that turns source text into kernel-checked terms, and offers two engines for it. The kernel reduces the term directly through 

decide +kernel ; the tactic native_decide instead compiles the function to native code and runs it, trading the kernel’s slow reduction for speed. The two engines differ in a theorem’s trusted base , the parts of the system whose cor-rectness it relies on, as reported by #print axioms . The baseline for any Lean development, the standard library and Mathlib included, is the kernel together with three standard ax-ioms: propositional extensionality ( propext ), quotient soundness ( Quot.sound ), and choice (Classical.choice ). Reduction through decide +kernel stays at this baseline. Evaluation through native_decide records one further native_decide axiom, which adds the Lean compiler to the trusted base. Both engines are available in LRAT-Catcher, and we return to this choice in Section 3. 

# 3 From a Certificate to a Theorem 

Let us fix the proof of a Schur-number upper bound as a concrete target. The Schur number 

S(k) is the largest n for which {1, . . . , n } admits a k-coloring with no monochromatic solution of a + b = c, a Schur-free k-coloring. We aim at the bound S(3) ≤ 13 : the set {1, . . . , 14 }

admits no Schur-free 3-coloring. In Lean, this is the theorem 

G = ¬ hasKSchurFreeColoring 3 14 .

We compare the ways to reach G; every workflow proves it through the same skeleton. 

• Encode. A verified encoding turns the problem into a CNF formula F ; for instance 

F = encodeK 3 14 , the formula asserting that {1, . . . , 14 } has a Schur-free 3-coloring, and the generator lratcatch-gen writes its DIMACS file for the solver. 

• Solve. CaDiCaL refutes F and emits an LRAT certificate π.

• Import. A workflow turns the certificate π into the Lean fact F. Unsat (that F is unsatisfiable). This step is problem-agnostic: it depends only on F and π, not on G

or the problem behind it. The workflows differ only here, and in what it costs in trust. 3• Lift. The encoding-soundness lemma, proved once by hand, lifts F. Unsat to the 

Lean 4 theorem G. Its proof shows that every Schur-free 3-coloring satisfies F , so an unsatisfiable F admits none. This proved correspondence, not the certificate, ties the formula to the problem; a faulty encoding could not be proved sound. The four workflows below realize the import: two are external alternatives, and two are LRAT-Catcher’s own reflection modes. (A further option, where the user asserts F. Unsat as an axiom on CaDiCaL ’s UNSAT verdict alone, discards the certificate and any independent guarantee; we set it aside.) 

Mathlib’s lrat_proof .

1. From F and π, the command’s elaboration builds an explicit proof term that witnesses unsatisfiability step by step. 2. The kernel checks this term, yielding F. Unsat .

Trusted base: the Lean kernel and its three standard axioms. The command does not implement the full RAT rule, and because the proof term embeds the whole formula, its memory grows with the formula and is exhausted on large instances. 

External checking with cake_lpr. 

1. The HOL4-verified checker cake_lpr validates π against the DIMACS file, outside Lean. 2. On success the user asserts F. Unsat in Lean as an axiom; there is no proof term. 

Trusted base: the kernel, cake_lpr ’s external verification, and the asserted axiom; the user must trust that cake_lpr genuinely ran and accepted. The result is a verdict turned into an axiom, not a proof the kernel has seen, as in the Empty Hexagon development [9]. 

LRAT-Catcher’s kernel reflection ( decide +kernel ). 

1. The tactic decide +kernel reduces the formally verified core checker on F and π inside the kernel. 2. Its soundness theorem yields F. Unsat .

Trusted base: the kernel and its three standard axioms, the same base as lrat_proof ,now with the full RAT rule. Kernel reduction is slow, which confines this mode to small certificates. 

LRAT-Catcher’s native reflection ( native_decide ). 

1. The tactic native_decide compiles the same verified checker to native code and runs it on F and π.2. Its soundness theorem yields F. Unsat , recording one native_decide axiom. 

Trusted base: the kernel, the three standard axioms, and the Lean compiler (the single 

native_decide axiom). This is the only workflow that scales to the certificates on which 

lrat_proof exhausts memory, at the price of trusting the compiler. All four yield the same Lean 4 theorem G; they differ only in the proof and its trusted base. The two modes of LRAT-Catcher are the extremes a user dials between: kernel reflec-tion adds nothing beyond the kernel but is slow, while native reflection adds the compiler yet scales. lrat_proof shares the kernel-only base but does not scale; cake_lpr scales but returns an axiom rather than a checked proof. Section 6 quantifies these differences. 44 Importing in LRAT-Catcher 

The two LRAT-Catcher workflows above are not separate commands but two modes of a single family of import commands. Each command turns a formula and a certificate into a Lean theorem; it runs native reflection by default and kernel reflection under a +kernel flag, at the two trusted bases compared above. The commands themselves differ only in what they read. 

From a file. The basic command imports a DIMACS file and an LRAT certificate as a Lean theorem. Given a formula file f.cnf and a certificate f.lrat , the invocation 

> lrat_reflect myProof "f.cnf" "f.lrat"

elaborates to a theorem of the form 

> myProof : (parseDimacs "p␣cnf␣3␣...").Unsat

whose statement contains the entire DIMACS file as a string literal, applied to parseDimacs ,the auditable DIMACS parser. The statement, therefore, commits to exactly one formula, the one obtained by applying that parser to the verbatim file contents. The reader can inspect its definition. Neither the certificate nor the checker appears in the statement. The proof term is checkLrat_sound applied to evidence that the Boolean function checkLrat 

returns true on the two strings; establishing that equation is exactly what reflection does. The parser is deliberately lenient. This keeps it short and auditable. To ensure that its leniency cannot let the statement and the file disagree, an elaboration-time validator inspects the DIMACS text first and rejects any line that is not a comment, a header, or a clause of integer tokens, and any file whose final token is not the clause terminator 0.

From a Lean formula. A second command imports a certificate against a formula already defined in Lean rather than read from a file. The invocation 

> lrat_reflect_cnf myProof (myFormula) "f.lrat"

yields myProof : myFormula.Unsat , with no parser in the statement, for any CNF myFormula 

already defined in Lean. The imported theorem is then an ordinary lemma about that formula, ready to feed into further reasoning about whatever it encodes. Section 6 uses this form, with myFormula the output of a verified encoding function. 

Solving during elaboration. When no certificate is at hand, lrat_decide myProof " f.cnf" runs the SAT solver during elaboration, writes a certificate to a temporary file, and imports it exactly as lrat_reflect does. The solver defaults to CaDiCaL and is overridable through the LRATCATCHER_SOLVER environment variable. It is invoked with –lrat –no-binary , and on CaDiCaL 3 also with –no-factor . The –no-factor flag is required because the factoring introduced in that version adds extension variables whose certificates the core checker soundly rejects. The resulting theorem has the same form as the one from 

> lrat_reflect

. Only the certificate’s provenance differs. 

Kernel mode. Kernel reduction is not free. The kernel cannot evaluate the string parser, so a kernel-mode statement embeds the parsed formula as an explicit CNF Nat literal, the CNF type over natural-number variables, rather than as parseDimacs applied to the file. More substantially, several constructs in the core checker are opaque to kernel reduction, such as well-founded recursion and hash-based clause storage. We supply kernel-reducible replacements and prove them equal to the originals, so that the soundness theorem of the core checker transfers unchanged, and the full RAT rule is preserved. Kernel mode, therefore, 5runs the same checker at a smaller trusted base, with speed as its only cost, and that cost restricts it to small certificates, as Section 6 quantifies. 

# 5 Cube-and-Conquer in Lean 

A cube-and-conquer run [4] splits a hard formula into many subproblems that can be solved independently. A set of cubes, each a partial assignment, divides the search space. Each leaf 

is the conjunction of a cube with the base formula. The solver refutes each leaf on its own, emitting one LRAT certificate per leaf. Reassembling these refutations into one statement about the base formula requires two ingredients: every leaf must be unsatisfiable, and the cubes must cover every assignment. The first is a list of certificate checks. The second is a combinatorial completeness condition that a naive composition would discharge with a trusted combinator. The rest of the development would then have to trust that combinator. 

The composition theorem. LRAT-Catcher composes the run entirely in Lean, without such a combinator, and relies on a single theorem, proved once and reused for every run. 

Theorem 5.1 (Cube-and-conquer composition) . Let F be a formula and C a finite set of cubes. If C ∧ F is unsatisfiable for every C ∈ C and the cubes cover every assignment, then 

F is unsatisfiable. 

Cover completeness as a certificate. The key is that cover completeness is itself a satisfiability question. Whenever the negated-cubes formula is unsatisfiable, the cubes cover every assignment. The negated-cubes formula contains one clause per cube, asserting that the cube is false. We establish the unsatisfiability of this formula with an LRAT certificate of its own, obtained by running the solver on it like any other leaf. Cover completeness, therefore, enters the proof not as an axiom or a trusted procedure but as one more checked certificate. Theorem 5.1 closes the gap between the leaf refutations and the conclusion. 

The composition command. A single Boolean function combines both ingredients. It checks every leaf certificate against its leaf and the cover certificate against the negated-cubes formula. It returns true only when all of them pass. Its soundness theorem yields 

> F.Unsat

. The command 

> lrat_cover_reflect myProof "base.cnf" "cubes.icnf" "leaf/" "cover.lrat"

reads the leaf certificates from the given prefix, runs that check by reflection, and emits 

> myProof : (parseDimacs "...").Unsat

. The statement names only the base formula. The cubes, the leaves, and every certificate feed the reflected check; none of them appears in the statement. If a cube set fails to cover or a leaf certificate fails to refute, the check fails rather than yielding an unsound theorem. The base and cube files pass through auditable parsers and elaboration-time validators, just as the single-certificate commands of Section 4 do. A companion command lrat_cover_reflect_cnf takes a Lean-defined formula in place of the base file. It produces myProof : F.Unsat for a CNF term F. This is the form used for the verified encodings of Section 6. An exporter prepares the certificate files. It emits the leaf formulas and the negated-cubes formula from the base and the cube file. The check runs with Lean’s heartbeat limit disabled, since a single run can contain more than a thousand leaves (Section 6). 6Building the leaves in parallel. The leaves are independent, so their certificates can be checked in separate processes. The tool lratcatch-cover-parallel emits one Lean module per cube, each running the reflected check for its own leaf, one module for the cover certifi-cate, and a final module that combines them by Theorem 5.1. The final module applies the theorem as an ordinary proof term and runs no reflected check of its own, so building the module set with lake checks the leaves concurrently, one process per module. The gain is a bound on memory, not a wall-clock figure (the latter depends on the cores lake is given). Each process holds a single leaf, so its peak memory is the cost of one reflected check on the largest leaf certificate, independent of the number of cubes and of the total certificate volume. A monolithic check instead loads the whole run into a single evaluation, where memory grows with the run. The split records one native_decide axiom per module in place of the monolithic command’s single axiom, on the same three standard axioms. 

# 6 Evaluation 

We evaluate the cost of importing certificates against the alternatives. We carry the running Schur example to its hardest instance and add the Ramsey numbers as a second example. 

The Schur and Ramsey encodings. The Schur encoding encodeK k n uses one variable per element and color, a clause forcing each element to take a color, and clauses forbidding each Schur triple a + b = c from being monochromatic. Its soundness lemma (Section 3) reads, in general, that if encodeK k n is unsatisfiable, then {1, . . . , n } has no Schur-free 

k-coloring. A witness checker confirms a coloring of {1, . . . , n } for the matching lower bound. We define schurNumber k n as the conjunction of the two bounds, that is S(k) = n,and import both S(3) = 13 and S(4) = 44 .Our Ramsey encoding uses the asymmetric formulation. The Ramsey number R(s, t )

is the least n such that every red-blue coloring of the edges of Kn contains a red Ks or a blue Kt. The encoding encode n s t has one variable per edge, a clause ruling out a red 

Ks on each s-subset of vertices, and a clause ruling out a blue Kt on each t-subset. Its soundness theorem reads R(s, t ) ≤ n, and the witness checker, justified by a completeness lemma for the enumerated subsets, certifies a coloring of Kn−1 for the lower bound. We define ramseyNumber s t n as R(s, t ) = n and import R(3 , 3) = 6 together with R(4 , 4) = 18 ,whose lower bound is the Paley coloring of K17 .

Scaling study. Beyond these two results, we evaluate how the import scales. A scaling study needs certificates of steadily growing size, which the Schur and Ramsey examples alone do not provide. We therefore benchmark on a ladder of pigeonhole formulas php (m+1 , m ),a standard stress test whose certificate size grows sharply with m, together with the Schur instances. On these we compare the four workflows of Section 3, discharging the same certificate check four ways. Because Mathlib ’s lrat_proof checks only RUP and not the RAT rule, the four-way comparison uses certificates that need no RAT step. We ran all four workflows on one machine, under Lean v4.30.0 with CaDiCaL 3.0.0, cake_lpr , and LRAT-Catcher v0.1.0. Hence, the times are comparable. # 7 Conclusion 

With full RAT support, LRAT-Catcher turns a DIMACS formula and an LRAT certificate into a Lean theorem on the verified Lean-core checker. A choice between a native checking mode and a kernel-only mode fixes how much of the system a user must trust. It composes cube-and-conquer runs entirely inside Lean. That the cubes cover every assignment is itself a checked certificate rather than a trusted combinator. Its verified encodings carry the im-ported refutations back to statements about the original problems. This is witnessed here by Schur and Ramsey number computations. Native reflection scales to certificates where explicit-proof-term import exhausts memory. The in-Lean composition delivers results be-yond the reach of any monolithic certificate. LRAT-Catcher makes a solver’s certificate a first-class theorem, available for reuse and composition. This narrows the distance between what SAT solvers decide and what proof assistants can state. PBLean [10] is a companion tool that aims to achieve the same goal for pseudo-Boolean (VeriPB) proof certificates. We expect this bridge to serve other combinatorial questions whose resolution rests on large unsatisfiability certificates.
